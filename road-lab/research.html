Research

Investigating how motivation and opportunity shape the paths we take through adulthood.

01
Primary Focus

Goal Selection & Pursuit

How do individuals choose goals that matter to them? How do they selectively direct their motivational and emotional resources toward chosen targets, and what happens when life circumstances change? The ROAD Lab explores these questions through the lens of major life transitions—career advancement, retirement, health challenges—where goal priorities must be reassessed and strategies adapted.

Much of this work focuses on the work domain, the transition to retirement, and how age-related perceptions affect goal selection. We also examine the role of perceived control: beliefs about whether outcomes are attainable strongly predict whether people maintain or disengage from pursuits. Drawing on Heckhausen's Motivational Theory of Life-Span Development (OPS theory), we study the dynamic balance between goal selection, engaged pursuit, and graceful disengagement as individuals navigate changing opportunities and constraints.

Key Themes

  • Life transitions and goal reorientation
  • Perceived control and motivational persistence
  • Selective engagement and strategic disengagement
  • Work, career, and retirement contexts
  • Age differences in motivation and goal pursuit
02
Primary Focus

Social Mobility

Beliefs matter. How individuals understand opportunity, merit, and fairness shapes their career expectations, motivation, and responses to setback. The ROAD Lab investigates how young adults calibrate their ambitions to labor market realities, how they sustain motivation toward uncertain socioeconomic goals, and how structural inequality interacts with personal agency.

We examine the transition to adulthood as a critical developmental window where educational and career pathways diverge. During this period, individuals form beliefs about whether hard work "pays off," whether opportunity exists for people like them, and whether they have what it takes to succeed. These beliefs affect not only goal selection but also resilience in the face of rejection and the willingness to continue investing in long-term pursuits. Our research highlights how socioeconomic context—family resources, neighborhood characteristics, educational access—shapes both objective opportunity and subjective beliefs about what is possible.

Key Themes

  • Beliefs about merit and opportunity
  • SES goal pursuit and career expectations
  • Emerging adulthood and life path divergence
  • Motivation in the face of structural barriers
  • Causal beliefs and perceived fairness
03
Primary Focus

Social Relationships

We do not develop in isolation. Close relationships—with family, friends, romantic partners, and community members—provide emotional support, practical help, shared motivation, and a sense of belonging. The ROAD Lab studies how relationships shape individual development and how individual goals and pursuits, in turn, affect relationship quality and satisfaction.

This research spans the lifespan from late adolescence through later adulthood, examining diverse relationship contexts. We explore how parents influence their children's career aspirations and academic engagement. We investigate the role of romantic partners in shared goal pursuit and life planning. We study how friendships and family bonds sustain well-being during life transitions and health challenges. And we examine community involvement and the sense of agency that comes from contributing to something larger than oneself. Throughout, we recognize that relationships are bidirectional: they shape us, and we shape them through our choices, values, and pursuits.

Key Themes

  • Family influence on career and educational goals
  • Romantic relationships and shared agency
  • Friendship, support, and well-being
  • Community involvement and belonging
  • Relationship quality across the lifespan

Theoretical Foundation

The Motivational Theory of Life-Span Development (OPS Theory)

The ROAD Lab is grounded in Jutta Heckhausen's Motivational Theory of Life-Span Development, which provides a dynamic framework for understanding how individuals navigate opportunities and constraints across adulthood. The theory emphasizes that development is not uniform—different life domains open and close at different ages, creating "windows of opportunity" for pursuing various goals.

Central to OPS theory is the insight that adaptive development requires not just persistence but also strategic disengagement. When goals become unrealistic due to changing circumstances, shifting one's effort to more attainable targets is psychologically healthy and motivationally adaptive. This perspective helps explain individual differences in well-being: people who can selectively engage with feasible goals and disengage from unrealistic ones show better health outcomes than those who perseverate on unattainable targets.

Lifespan Developmental Perspective

Our work also draws on broader lifespan developmental psychology, which emphasizes that development continues throughout life, that different periods present different challenges and opportunities, and that individual agency interacts with contextual constraints. We recognize that people are not passive recipients of their circumstances—they actively interpret opportunity, make choices about their goals, and work to shape their futures. At the same time, we acknowledge that structural factors—inequality, discrimination, limited access to education or healthcare—constrain the choices available to different groups.

Looking Forward

The ROAD Lab's current and emerging priorities include:

  • Healthy Aging. How do motivational strategies earlier in life affect health and well-being in later adulthood? Can interventions that teach strategic goal disengagement and reorientation promote resilience when facing health challenges?
  • Socioeconomic Inequality. How do structural barriers and systemic discrimination affect goal selection and motivational persistence? What individual and contextual factors help people sustain hope and effort in the face of unequal opportunity?
  • Relationships in Crisis. How do major life disruptions—health diagnoses, job loss, forced migration—affect romantic relationships and family dynamics? Can relationship-based interventions support motivation and well-being during turbulent transitions?
